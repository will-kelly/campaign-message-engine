# Worked example — complete campaign

A full run of the Campaign Message Engine, from intake to output. This file serves two purposes: it shows the buyer what good looks like, and the engine uses it as a pattern-match reference for output quality.

---

## The intake

**Core message:**
> Our customer onboarding software cuts time-to-first-value from 6 weeks to 9 days for B2B SaaS companies with complex products.

**Audience:**
Heads of customer success and VPs of customer experience at B2B SaaS companies with $5M–$50M ARR. They own the onboarding metric. They have 3–15 customer success managers reporting to them. They're under board pressure to reduce churn and increase net revenue retention.

**Voice (three descriptors):**
- Plain-spoken
- Data-forward
- Slightly impatient with customer success theater

**Desired action:**
Book a 20-minute demo.

**Off-limits:**
- Don't name specific competitor products
- Don't claim specific churn reduction numbers (we don't have audited data)
- Don't use "delight" or "wow moment" — the audience hates these terms

## Voice consistency note (from the engine)

All six channels carry the same specific claim — 73% configuration time, 6 weeks to 9 days, 47-flow teardown. The "customer success theater" voice descriptor shows up as direct pushback on industry clichés (no "wow moment," no "delight," explicit refusal to recommend hiring as the fix). Plain-spoken voice maintained through contractions, short sentences, and direct address. The sales email and LinkedIn post run sharper than the landing page and email because organic channels reward stronger opinions; paid copy compressed the same argument to fit character limits without softening it.

## What to notice in this example

If you're benchmarking your own output against this:

1. **The number 73% appears in five of six channels.** The same proof point carries across surfaces. The engine should pull a single piece of evidence and reuse it, not invent new evidence per channel.

2. **No channel uses "wow moment," "delight," or "transform your customer experience."** Voice constraints held under pressure to fill space.

3. **The CTA varies by channel commitment.** Email and ads point to a demo. LinkedIn ends with a question. Sales email asks for a 15-min walkthrough specifically. Video drops a link in comments. Each CTA matches the channel's friction level.

4. **The H1 names the outcome, not the product.** "Cut B2B SaaS onboarding from 6 weeks to 9 days" — not "Welcome to Customer Onboarding Co."

5. **The sales email references something specific about the recipient's company** (the team hitting 12 reports). The engine flagged this as a placeholder; you'd fill it in per recipient. But the structure — specific reference → claim → low-friction ask — is the pattern.

6. **No exclamation points anywhere.** The voice descriptor "plain-spoken, data-forward, slightly impatient" doesn't allow for them.

7. **The video script doesn't open with a greeting.** It opens mid-sentence with the strongest line.

If your output looks structurally different from this — different argument across channels, varying CTAs that don't ladder up, voice that drifts between pieces — your voice guide or core message isn't doing enough work.
