# Channel specifications

Reference document for the Campaign Message Engine. Upload to the Claude project's knowledge base. The engine pulls from this when adapting a core message.

---

## 1. Email (marketing or nurture)

**Purpose:** the audience already opted in. You're earning continued attention, not buying it. Email rewards relevance over cleverness.

**Length:** 80–150 words in the body. Anything longer and the reader skims. Anything shorter and the CTA feels unearned.

**Subject lines:** 3 variants, each under 50 characters. Test these patterns:
- The promise ("The 9-day onboarding teardown")
- The curiosity ("What broke our customer success calls")
- The direct ("New: faster onboarding for complex SaaS")

**Preheader text:** 1 line, around 80 characters. It complements the subject — never repeats it. If the subject is the hook, the preheader is the proof.

**Body structure:**
- Open with one specific sentence that hooks. Not "I hope this email finds you well."
- Middle: the argument, compressed. One example or one data point if relevant.
- Close: the action. One CTA, hyperlinked, written as a phrase ("see the breakdown") not a button word ("click here").

**Sign-off:** matches voice. First name only for personal-brand emails. Brand name for company emails.

**Common failures:**
- The "warm-up paragraph" before the real point
- Two CTAs competing for the click
- Subject line and body that don't tell the same story
- Bullet lists for content that should be prose

## 2. LinkedIn post (organic)

**Purpose:** distribution and signal. The reader is scrolling. You have two lines to stop the scroll.

**Length:** 150–250 words. Posts under 100 words feel like filler. Posts over 300 lose readers in the truncation.

**Hook structure:** the first two lines are everything. They appear above the "see more" fold. They must do one of:
- Stake a clear position
- Name a specific tension
- Open a loop the reader wants closed

Bad hooks: "I've been thinking about content marketing lately." "Here's something I learned this week." "I want to share a story with you."

Good hooks: "Most customer onboarding software solves a problem nobody has." "Six weeks to first value is not an onboarding issue. It's a product issue."

**Body structure:**
- After the hook, 2–4 short paragraphs that develop the argument
- Use line breaks generously — every 1–2 sentences
- One concrete example or data point if available
- End with a question, an invitation, or a soft CTA. Not a hard pitch.

**Hashtags:** 0–3, only if the voice guide uses them. Trailing hashtag chains look like 2017.

**Common failures:**
- Generic openers ("Hot take:" "Real talk:" "Unpopular opinion:")
- Engagement bait ("Agree? Drop a 🔥 below")
- The same insight stretched across 12 lines for "thought leadership" effect
- Closing with "Thoughts?" when the post hasn't earned a response

## 3. Paid social ad copy (LinkedIn or Meta sponsored)

**Purpose:** buying attention from people who didn't ask for you. Every word costs money.

**Length:**
- Headline: max 70 characters. 2 variants.
- Primary text: 90–125 characters. This is the hook.
- Description: 1 line, max 30 characters. Frames the CTA.

**Tone:** one level more direct than your organic content. Paid copy has no setup. Lead with the benefit or the curiosity. Drop the warm-up.

**CTA button:** pick from the platform's standard options. Don't invent custom buttons that don't exist:
- LinkedIn: Learn More, Sign Up, Download, Get Quote, Apply Now, Subscribe, Register, Visit Website
- Meta: Learn More, Sign Up, Get Offer, Book Now, Contact Us, Download, Subscribe, Apply Now

**Visual partnership:** paid copy almost always pairs with an image, video, or carousel. The copy should not duplicate the visual — they should reinforce each other. Note in the output what kind of visual would support the copy.

**Common failures:**
- Headlines that try to be clever instead of clear
- Primary text that buries the offer in throat-clearing
- CTA buttons that don't match the offer ("Learn More" for a free trial signup)
- Generic stock claims ("Trusted by industry leaders")

## 4. Landing page hero (above-the-fold)

**Purpose:** the reader landed here from an ad, an email, or a referral. They have one question: am I in the right place? The hero must answer in under three seconds.

**H1:** 6–12 words. The single sharpest version of the core message. State the outcome or the category, not the brand.
- Weak: "Welcome to ProductName"
- Strong: "Cut B2B onboarding from 6 weeks to 9 days"

**Subhead:** 15–25 words. Adds the "for whom" and "so that." Closes the gap between the headline and the CTA.
- Weak: "We help companies improve their customer journey"
- Strong: "For B2B SaaS companies with complex products. Get your customers to value before they churn."

**Primary CTA button:** 2–4 words. Action verb + object. Specific.
- Weak: "Get Started" "Learn More"
- Strong: "Book a 20-min demo" "See the playbook"

**Secondary CTA (optional):** 2–4 words. Lower commitment. For the visitor who isn't ready to convert.
- "Watch the 90-sec tour"
- "Read the case study"

**Trust strip:** what sits under the CTA — logos, social proof line, guarantee, or stat. The engine should specify which type would work, not invent fake logos or fabricate stats. Example output: "Trust strip recommendation: 3–5 customer logos relevant to the target ICP, plus a single-line stat if available."

**Common failures:**
- H1 that names the product instead of the outcome
- Subhead that repeats the H1 in different words
- CTA buttons that hide the commitment ("Get Started" → starts a signup flow)
- Fabricated social proof or made-up customer logos

## 5. Sales email (1:1 outbound)

**Purpose:** start a conversation with a specific person who has never heard of you. Earn one reply, not a meeting.

**Length:** 50–90 words. The shorter the better. Long sales emails get archived.

**Subject lines:** 3 variants, max 40 characters. Conversational. Lowercase is fine. Banned:
- "Quick question"
- "Touching base"
- "Following up"
- "[First name]?"

Better patterns:
- A specific reference to their work or company
- A direct claim about their problem
- A short question that earns a reply

**Body structure (three parts):**

1. **Relevance line** — why I'm writing them specifically. Reference their work, their company, a trigger event. NOT a flattery line ("Loved your recent post"). A signal that you actually read something or did research.

2. **Value or claim** — the core message, compressed to one sentence. What's in it for them, not what your company does.

3. **The ask** — one specific request, low friction. Never "30-minute call to discuss." Better:
   - "Worth a 15-min call next Tuesday or Thursday?"
   - "Open to me sending a 2-paragraph teardown of how this would work for [Company]?"
   - "Reply 'no' and I won't follow up — reply with a question and I will."

**Sign-off:** first name only. No "Best regards," no "Sincerely." No P.S. unless the voice guide explicitly uses them.

**Common failures:**
- Long preamble before the actual reason for the email
- "I'd love to learn more about your business" (no one offers their time for free)
- Asking for a 30-min meeting on the first touch
- Mentioning the sender's company before the recipient's problem

## 6. Short-form video hook (LinkedIn video, Reels, Shorts)

**Purpose:** stop the scroll, hold attention for 45–60 seconds, get one action.

**Length:** target 45–60 seconds of total runtime. Under 30 seconds rarely lands a point. Over 90 seconds loses retention.

**First 3 seconds:** one spoken line, max 12 words. This is the pattern interrupt. The visual should show the speaker mid-sentence — not a logo, not an intro card.

Strong opening lines:
- "Most onboarding software solves a problem you don't have."
- "Six weeks to first value isn't an onboarding issue."
- "I tore down 47 customer onboarding flows last year."

Weak opening lines:
- "Hi everyone, today I want to talk about..."
- "So I've been thinking about this thing..."
- "Welcome back to my channel."

**Setup (15–20 seconds, 2–3 lines):**
- Establish the tension or the stakes
- Reference a specific situation or pattern
- Earn the right to the payoff

**Payoff (15–20 seconds, 2–3 lines):**
- Deliver the insight or the claim
- One concrete example or proof point
- Don't soften it with caveats

**CTA (5–10 seconds):**
- Spoken AND on-screen text
- Specific action: "Link in the first comment." "DM me 'playbook' for the breakdown." "Book a 20-min call — link in my bio."

**Format conventions:**
- Write as a script with the spoken text plain
- Speaker direction in [brackets] only when needed for clarity: [hold up document], [cut to screen recording]
- Note the on-screen text separately when it differs from the spoken text

**Common failures:**
- Intros that waste the first 5 seconds
- Setup that runs longer than the payoff
- CTAs that aren't actually possible (telling people to "click below" on LinkedIn video, which doesn't have a clickable area)
- Reading the post aloud instead of writing for spoken delivery

## Cross-channel rules

These apply to every channel:

1. **Same argument, different surface.** The claim in the email body is the same claim in the LinkedIn hook is the same claim in the H1. Channel norms change. The argument doesn't.

2. **Voice doesn't soften across channels.** If the brand voice is dry and direct, the paid ad shouldn't suddenly use exclamation points. If the brand voice is warm and consultative, the sales email shouldn't go cold and transactional.

3. **One CTA per piece.** Two CTAs equals zero CTAs. Pick the one action that matters and write toward it.

4. **No filler.** No "in today's world." No "we're excited to announce." No "stay tuned." If a sentence could be cut without losing the argument, cut it.

5. **Specificity beats abstraction.** "60% of clients" beats "many clients." "B2B SaaS, 500–5,000 employees" beats "growing companies." Numbers, names, time periods, and dollar amounts all increase credibility.
